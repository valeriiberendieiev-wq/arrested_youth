What they have in common

The key pattern in the data: each track goes through an absolutely identical development model: peak → active playlists → exit from rotation → cyclical fall in daily streams from -65% to -88%. Tracks lose momentum not because of the material's quality, but because of the specifics of the streaming ecosystem: without a regular second wave of marketing support or viral triggers, Spotify algorithms automatically shift attention to new releases.

Analytics for each track

  • The Kid I Used to Know (−65% from peak, ~31.8K/day) – The main locomotive and most powerful back-catalog. Despite the deep fall in 2025 after washing out of top editorial lists, the track retains a solid ~31.8K streams daily. Needs a complete reboot through external sources, as internal Spotify tools have already "fallen asleep" for it.

BRIEF CONCLUSIONS

  • Gap between eras: The channel has an overwhelmingly powerful back-catalog from 2017–2019 (The Kid I Used to Know, 98 Degrees, VANS), but 2024–2025 releases gather 20–40 times fewer views. This is a classic "platform effect without a strategy" — the quality is there, the promo is not.
  • Collaborations are underestimated: "Find My Own Way (feat. Mark Hoppus)" — a collab with a blink-182 legend — gathered only 241K views in 4 years. This is catastrophically low for such a partnership. An estimated 559K potential views were lost.
  • Shorts — a missed opportunity: New videos (Little Cup, Walked Out, Free Now) have strong visual potential, but no Shorts content was made from them. This correlates directly with low metrics.
  • Nostalgia engine is working: The track "The Kid I Used to Know" (2019) with 864K views continues to attract new audiences — proving that the nostalgic/coming-of-age theme is a key entry point for new listeners.

RECOMMENDATIONS

  • Priority 1 — Shorts resuscitation of new releases: Little Cup and Walked Out in the Middle of the Night are priority tracks for Shorts. Both have official videos with cinematic footage. Cut 15–30 second vertical videos with text on screen. Free Now (Official Video) is an ideal candidate for viral Shorts. Bright red-and-white design and dynamic editing — an exact hit for TikTok/Reels/Shorts algorithms.
  • Priority 2 — Reactivation of the Mark Hoppus collab: "Find My Own Way" received catastrophically little promo for such a partnership. Make a Short with an emphasis: "We recorded a song with a member of blink-182" — a nostalgic trigger for the 25–35 audience. Use Mark Hoppus's name in Bio and titles as social proof in the international market.
  • Priority 3 — Nostalgic content around the back catalog: "The Kid I Used to Know" — 6 years ago this track gathered 864K views. Make a Shorts series in a "then vs now" format or behind-the-scenes to redirect old traffic to new content. Similarly for "98 Degrees" and "VANS" — these tracks are still in an active YouTube recommendation cycle, providing a free entry point for new subscribers.
  • Priority 4 — Next big release with a promo strategy: No track after 2020 has exceeded 100K views — this is not a quality problem, it's a release strategy problem. The next release must come with: (1) a Shorts campaign 2–3 weeks before release, (2) an official music video simultaneously with the release, (3) cross-posting on Instagram/TikTok.

3.2 Search for a sleeping hit - a potential TikTok hit that can be resuscitated with a push

Currently, there is no single obvious sleeping hit that has almost become a trend and only needs one push. The situation looks like this: several tracks have already shown their potential and audience response, even without any TikTok strategy, content, or media push. Meaning, the music resonated with people. Tracks that have gained the highest number of listens are already potential tracks for resuscitation. Meaning, these tracks gained usage not through a push mechanic, but because the audience themselves found a use for them in their content.

The task now is not to "find one magic track". But to reactivate already proven popular sounds and give them a second lifecycle through the proper use of promotion channels. In fact, these tracks have already proven they can live on TikTok, nobody just helped them grow further.

None of the tracks received:
  • its own trend;
  • a systemic UGC push;
  • a TikTok strategy;
  • a repeated re-push;
  • personal participation of the artist in the content.

Because of this, even strong sounds stopped at the early organic stage instead of transitioning to massive algorithmic growth.

Each of these tracks needs:
  • a defined usage format;
  • an explanation of the track's meaning to the audience;
  • a repeatable template;
  • clear logic for use;
  • stimulation for fans to use the sound;
  • building a trend around an emotion or situation.

TikTok does not promote songs. TikTok promotes scenarios for using songs.
